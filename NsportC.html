NsportC App

In September 2016, the client reached out to my team seeking design solutions for his product - NsportC. NsportC is a combination of web and mobile app that helps middle school and high school coaches handle potential concussions in the right way. Furthermore, NsportC will also help teenager sports players and their parents keep pre-medical information.

My Role

I led a team of 6 UI/UX designers designing NsportC applications across iOS, Android, and Web since September 2016. The design team focused on discovering user requirements and creating delightful user experience.

Up until November 2016, I led efforts to evolve the design from basically nothing to hi-fidelity prototypes. In addition, I led the team to conduct market research and potential-user interviews to address user pain-points and ensure there is a market opportunity.

Three Months:
From Zero To Hero

In September 2016, our client approached us with two primary goals - to design the user interfaces that combines functionality and usability for their products; and to discover true user needs in order to help them promote the product and find business partners.

In order to let the development team start as early as possible and help the client catch this market opportunity, our team was under high pressure to move forward fast. We were aimed to deliver both the website design and mobile app design by the start of December 2016, which meant we only had about 3 months to generate hi-fidelity prototypes. To ensure that we were able to provide high quality designs and beat the deadlines at the same time, I created a project plan to set milestone goals.

What is
"In Suspicion" of Concussion

We started the discovery phase quickly. Since this product is related to teenager sports players' health, there might be legal limitations of what we can do. To understand what the legal requirements are, our team researched on regulations of high school coaches' responsibilities. From our research, we found two major design constraints:

1. If a teenager player is in suspicion of having concussion, the coach should remove the player from play immediately.
2. Only medical professonals should determine whether a teenager player has concussion.

However, removing players and sending players to doctors once any incidents happen seemed to be unrealistic. Unlike many other injuries, concussion often cannot be determined by a player's physical situation. So our assumption is that, many times, coaches are unsure about the possibility of concussion if a teenager player gets hit in the head or fall to the floor. If our assumption is right, then the purpose of our product is to become an assessment tool that helps coaches determine whether a player should be suspected of having concussion.

User Intervews & Personas

We kicked off the user research right after the discovery phase. To confirm our assumption, we interviewed with two coaches, Joe and Max. Both of them have been high school coaches for over 10 years, and they have children that play in high school teams. Both as coaches and parents, they talked about their experience handling teenager player incidents and what they expect from our product. From our user interviews, we confirmed our assumption and realized that our product could potentially help both coaches and players' parents. Here are some major take-ins:

1. Our assumption is correct. Coaches can hardly tell whether a player should be "suspected" to have concussion.
2. Although state laws regulates that coaches must go through a concussion training, but only a few can remember the details of the training.
3. Parents think the pre-medical records are important even if the incident does not lead to concussion, because they believe that the real problem is the overall brain health, not how many concussions happened in the past.

After interviewing with coaches, we decided to design two persona types - primary persona for middle school or high school coaches and secondary persona for teenager players' parents - in order to prioritise who we will be focusing on in the early stage. We used personas constantly to make decisions, set priorities, and create user scenarios.

For our primary persona type, we developed two personas varying by their coaching experience. This variable could greatly affect the behavior and experience using our product. A more experienced coach may have more awareness of each player's health status, while a less experienced coach may miss important indicators that a player's health status is unstable. We also developed a secondary persona to represent teenager players' parents. We discussed the personas with our client to understand more of the big picture and started drawing workflow diagrams.

Visualize the Process:
Workflow Diagrams

Using our primary persona - coach's role - as a breakthrough point, we created workflow diagrams to visualize the users end-to-end experience and map the product workflow. The workflow diagram defines how different types of users interact with the product and how they collaborate to accomplish tasks, as well as how information flows between users and the product. Later we had a conversation about the workflow with our client to confirm that we were running towards the right direction.

Onboarding is the initial step for coaches to enjoy our product. While designing the registration process, we discovered that asking coaches to type in every player's demographical information may be too much, especially for coaches who manage multiple sports teams. To alleviate the burden on coaches, we ask parents to register and put in the demographic information for their children. At the same time, we are giving them access to all pre-medical records/test results. Once a coach add all players on the team, their parents will receive an email inviting them to register and type in players' information. Once a parent completes the registration process, his children will be officially registered.

After registering an account and creating a team, the coach will be directed to the Dashboard first where he or she can see all teams as well as any new changes or new records in the system. After selecting a team, the coach will be directed to the Player List where all players in the team will be shown. Players will be shown in 4 types of status - "not registered" means the player's parent has not completed the registration; "normal" means no signs of concussion; "in treatment" means a player is confirmed injured and cannot play; and "in observation" means a player is suspected of having concussion according to our assessment tool and needs to see a medical professional for further diagnosis.

The mobile application is a complimentary product to the web app. It covers all functions on the web app plus one additional function - the assessment tool. Since during games or practices, coaches do not have access to computers but have smartphones with them, we decided to put the assessment tool exclusively on the mobile app. Depending on the assessment results, the teenager will either considered concussion-free if they pass the assessment tests or considered "needs to see a doctor for further diagnosis" if the tests are failed.

Determine "In Suspicion"
Assessment Tool

Now our focus falls onto the word "in suspicion" again. We researched on concussion training materials and asked opinions from experienced coaches. To determine the mental state of a teenager player and the possibility of concussion, coaches normal ask three types of questions to the players when an incident happens:

1. Basic questions related to the player's demographical information. For example, "what is your mother's name?" and "what grade are you in?".
2. Cognitive questions to test the logical thinking of the player. For example, "can you repeat reversely of the numbers listed here?" and "can you tell me what is 3 times 5 minus 6?"
3. Motional tasks to see if the player can control the body balance. For example, "on a firm surface, stand on single foot" and "one foot behind other".

The answers from experienced coaches coincided with our client's research. Because timing is important to further treatment of concussion, coaches suggested the total assessment time should be less than 5 minutes.

First, once a player is registered completely on our product, either the coach or the player's parent will do a benchmark test to get the player's response in normal status. When an incident happens, the coach or the assistant coach will use our application to conduct the assessment test. Then our algorithm (produced by our client) will compare the assessment test results with the benchmark test results of the player to decide whether the test is successful or failed.

Structure the Experience:
Objectives & Wireframes

Understanding the features on the web and mobile app helped us develop a clear vision of user needs and expectations. We listed a set of experience principles to define the "personality" of our product. The principles used here were addressed to specific user needs including functionality and usability.

With the objectives in mind, our team gernerated a set of wireframes for the arrangement of UI components and interactions using Balsamiq Mockups. The wireframes were used to visualize and communicate the anatomy of both the web and mobile app to our clients and users.

The web app is mainly designed with emphasis on information management. Coaches will be able to view all players' status and previous assessment test results. In addition, coaches can print out reports based on his players' status.

The mobile app covers most of the functions on the web app, but it has the assessment tool exclusively. To simplify the process and save time, the coach will be able to quickly access the assessment tool on the main page and then select the player associated with the test. The coach will also be able to quickly switch teams via the sidebar and view players' status and information.

Simulate the Experience:
Hi-fidelity Prototype

I selected Sketch as our main design tool because of its easiness to use. Due to the tight deadlines we have, I divided the team into two small teams - web team and mobile team - each has three designers. Each team was working closely internally and communicating with the other team constantly in order to unify design styles. Fortunately Sketch has the ability to create symbols and layers to reuse UI components. After both small teams finished the first version of the hi-fidelity prototype design, all six designers discussed together and gave each other feedback for design iterations.

Simplify Registration

Coaches will be able to register through our website. The first step is to register with the coach's email address and create a password. Then the coach needs to create a sports team by typing in "team name" and "sports type". The last step is to add all player and their parents' email addresses. Our product will automatically email invitations to the parents for registering their children.

Parents will only be able to register through invitations sent by coaches. Once the parent clicks on the invitation link, the parent will be directed to the registration page where he or she needs to register with an email as well and type in the player's demographical information. Once all steps are completed, the player is successfully registered on NsportC system.

Streamline Information Flow

The dashboard page serves as a central information hub. The coach is able to view all teams as well as new changes or new test results. Furthermore, the coach is able to add or delete teams on this page.Clicking on any team, the coach will be directed to the team page of the team.

The team page shows a list of all players in this team as well as the team information. The coach can change the team's name and add/delete players on this page. "Not registered" player will be grayed out, but the coach can choose to resend an invitation to the player's parent.

Organize Player Information

A player's information can be divided into two parts - demographical information and test results. The coach will be able to edit a player's demographical information as well as view or export all previous test results. On the mobile application, the coach will be able to conduct assessment tests for the player. If the player is just registered, the coach or the parent should conduct the benchmark test; after completing the benchmark test, the coach will be able to run assessment test if an incident happens.

Standardize the Assessment

Once the assessment test started, a warning message will jump out telling the coach that "if the player is obviously injured, there is no need to do the test. The player needs to be taken to medical professionals as soon as possible". If the coach decides to continue with the test, then the application will ask the coach to check if the player has concussion symptoms. Then the assessment will walk the coach through basic questions, cognitive questions, and balance tasks, with specific instructions of how to ask (for example, "now I'm going to test your physical response, tell me know you got here today...")

After finishing all assessment questions with 3 minutes (less than 5 minutes), the mobile app will determine whether the test is passed or failed based on comparison between the assessment test and the player's benchmark test. If the test is failed, our product will advice the coach to remove the player from play and send the player to medical professionals for further diagnosis. There will also be a shortcut button to send an email to let the player's parent know the situation.

Refine the Experience:
Usability Studies

After finishing the hifi UI design, we built an interactive prototype using Justinmind Prototyper and conducted usability tests to fix usability issues. During the usability studies session, we asked 4 people that fit our personas to complete a set of tasks and listened to their feedback. Here are some examples of UI changes based on the usability studies results:

On our first version, we use the alphabetical order for the player list. According to user feedback, "order by status" is a more intuitive way for them to see who can play and who cannot.

When we test the transactions of pages, an actual coach pointed out that teenage player may look similar on small profile pictures, so we added player names to identify each player.
